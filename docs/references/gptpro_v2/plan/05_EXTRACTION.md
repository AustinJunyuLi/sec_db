# Extraction: Clean Text to Structured Intermediate

Extraction transforms clean text into evidence-backed candidates. The layer should prefer many precise candidates with explicit uncertainty over a small number of over-resolved facts. Reconciliation can merge or reject candidates, but it cannot recover facts that extraction never surfaced.

The first pass is actor and alias extraction. It identifies the target, winning acquirer, named bidders, anonymous bidders, advisors, legal counsel, shareholders, committees, financing sources, and regulators. It records alias definitions such as `Industry Participant`, `Party B`, `G&W`, `New Mountain Capital`, `Sponsor E`, and `Company I`. It also identifies group labels and potential member changes, such as the PetSmart Buyer Group, the Saks Sponsor A/Sponsor E/Sponsor G shifts, and bidder combinations in PetSmart. Actor candidates include the exact label, normalized label, actor type candidate, bidder subtype candidate, and evidence.

The second pass is event extraction. It scans for dated clauses and creates event candidates for board meetings, advisor engagements, outreach, confidentiality agreements, data-room access, management presentations, process letters, proposal submissions, draft agreement circulation, exclusivity, withdrawal, rejection, process termination, signing, announcement, go-shop start, and go-shop end. Event candidates retain rough date wording. `early July 2016` becomes a date interval with precision `month` or `relative`, not a false exact date.

The third pass is proposal and bid extraction. It recognizes per-share bids, ranges, aggregate values, enterprise values, equity values, verbal ceilings, non-cash consideration, CVRs, and mixed consideration. For a range such as `$81.00 to $83.00 per share`, `bid_value_lower` is 81.00, `bid_value_upper` is 83.00, and `bid_value` is the midpoint unless the filing identifies a single operative value. For `at least $15 per share`, store lower bound 15.00, upper bound null, operator `at_least` in `raw_note`, and bid center null unless the projection has an explicit rule. For `not above the current stock price of approximately $78`, store upper bound 78.00, raw note `ceiling`, and mark valuation comparability as low unless a center is justified.

Proposal event typing is critical. `proposal_submitted` is reserved for proposals eligible to enter the current bidder-cycle view. Incomplete unsolicited letters, expressions with no transaction terms, proposals to buy a different company, and reengagement attempts after a bidder has been removed from the process are stored as other proposal event types. This preserves the information without causing `derive_views.py` to misclassify a non-admitted bidder as admitted.

The fourth pass extracts process structure. It finds process launches, pauses, terminations, restarts, final-round invitations, confirmatory diligence shortlists, binding-offer deadlines, go-shop windows, and post-signing solicitations. It emits candidate cycle boundaries and candidate formal boundaries. A formal boundary candidate must be a transition that changes the bidder set or process obligations: invitation to a final round, request for final bids with draft merger agreement, grant of confirmatory diligence to a shortlist, or exclusivity for one bidder after comparative screening. Mere execution of an NDA or general data-room access to all initial participants is not sufficient by itself.

The fifth pass extracts dropout and screening evidence. It creates rejection candidates for language such as `not invited`, `eliminated`, `no longer involved`, or `unlikely to be competitive`. It creates voluntary-withdrawal candidates for `withdrew`, `declined`, `not interested`, `unable to proceed`, `elected not to submit`, or `stopped responding`. It creates ambiguity candidates when nonappearance is observed but the filing does not disclose who acted first. Each dropout candidate is linked to a bidder, cycle, and triggering evidence.

The sixth pass extracts auxiliary deal data. It records financial advisors, legal counsel, advisor changes, committee names, committee members, authority limits, independent-director status, go-shop length, termination fees, reverse termination fees, voting agreements, financing conditions, regulatory contingencies, standstill provisions, and prior relationships. Examples from the reference filings include PetSmart’s J.P. Morgan and Wachtell Lipton roles, Providence and Worcester’s GHF-to-BMO transition and Transaction Committee, Zep’s King & Spalding and 30-day go-shop, and Saks’ Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Wachtell Lipton, 40-day go-shop, and differentiated termination fees.

The seventh pass extracts aggregate counts. Filings often say that a number of parties were contacted, signed confidentiality agreements, submitted indications, or received data-room access without naming each party. The candidate layer must store both aggregate process counts and any individually named bidder candidates. Reconciliation later decides whether to create anonymous bidder actors for count completeness.

Extraction output is not allowed to silently normalize away uncertainty. Every candidate has confidence, evidence, and raw text. Candidate conflicts are expected: a bidder may have multiple aliases, a group may change members, a bid may be both a range and a verbal ceiling, and a cycle boundary may have competing candidate dates. These conflicts are resolved only in reconciliation through canonical records and judgments.

Alias extraction must preserve chronology. A group label can mean different members at different times. In Saks, Sponsor E first appears with Sponsor A, then with Sponsor G, then again with Sponsor A. In PetSmart, two bidders are later treated together as Bidder 3. The extractor should therefore create group-membership candidates with start evidence and end evidence rather than treating the final group as if it existed for the whole process.

Boundary extraction should produce multiple candidates when the text supports multiple plausible readings. A request for non-binding indications, a management-presentation stage, a request for markups, and a final-bid deadline can all appear in one process. Each candidate records the transition it represents, affected bidders, and whether the bidder set narrowed. Reconciliation chooses the boundary used by the current view but keeps rejected candidates for audit.

The extractor must also detect negative evidence. Phrases such as `no proposals were made`, `no party remained engaged`, `did not include financing`, `no details or further information`, and `no subsequent communications` are not empty text; they support scope-validity, valuation-comparability, and dropout judgments. Negative evidence candidates should be linked to the actor or cycle they qualify.

For board and committee data, extract both existence and authority. A committee that supervises and recommends is different from one that can approve a transaction. Providence and Worcester states that the Transaction Committee assisted supervision and recommendation but lacked authority to approve a change-of-control transaction. That distinction should be stored because future research may compare committee authority, not only committee presence.

For deal terms, extract numeric value, unit, trigger, and period. A termination fee is not only a dollar amount; it may apply after the go-shop or for a superior proposal. A go-shop term is not only length; it has a start date, end time, excluded-party rule, and sometimes a lower fee. These details should become term candidates with their own evidence rather than raw notes attached to the signing event.
